The People Behind the Numbers

These are not statistics. They are future physicians - people who chose to give an extra year of their training to teach others.

These are future physicians - people who chose to give an extra year of their training to teach others, believing they were building a stronger foundation for their careers and for the institutions that trained them. Here are some of their stories.

🚑

Megan Stacy

Anatomy Fellow

Former police officer and EMT who served the Greater New Orleans communities for 10 years as a first responder. Goal: Emergency medicine physician.

"

"My name is Megan Stacy, and as an incoming 4th year medical student, recent changes to federal financial aid guidelines have placed my completion of medical school at risk. Between my 2nd and 3rd years of medical school I participated in an additional year of training in my institution's pre-doctoral clinical anatomy fellowship. This fellowship experience allowed me to conduct research, expand my teaching skills, work ahead on my dual-degree master's in public health, and gain deep exposure to surgical skills."

With the addition of a 5th year to my education, I now no longer qualify for financial aid. These federal aid changes prevent me from taking out any form of loans next academic year - unsubsidized loans, subsidized loans, and GradPlus loans. Not only does this impact financing medical school, but GradPlus loans have been the method used to finance my dual-degree courses. These policy changes took effect on May 1st, giving students very little time to seek alternative funding.

- Megan Stacy, Student Doctor, KCU-COM

I can't ask my children to sacrifice their stability so I can finish medical school. But I also can't give up now, not when I'm this close to graduation.

- Student Doctor [Name], Student Doctor, KCU-COM

What Happens If This Goes Unfunded

Interrupted Education

Fellows who cannot qualify for private loans may be forced to interrupt or permanently withdraw from their medical programs - in their final year.

Unsustainable Debt

Those who do qualify for private loans will face unsustainable debt burdens during residency, with long-term consequences for financial stability, mental health, and practice location.

Systemic Impact

The precedent set will deter future students from pursuing pre-doctoral fellowships, reducing the pipeline of teaching-committed physicians.

The medical education community benefits when talented students dedicate a year of training to teaching and mentorship. When that dedication is met with financial punishment, the long-term effect is a less experienced, less pedagogically committed physician workforce.
